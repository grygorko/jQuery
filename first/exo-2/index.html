Joseph Dacre Carlyle (Carlisle, June 4, 1758 - Newcastle upon Tyne, April 12, 1804) is an English orientalist. After his studies at Cambridge University he became a professor of Arabic. In 1799 he was attached by the British government to Lord Elgin's embassy in the Ottoman Empire. He was in charge of exploring the libraries of the region (mainly that of the Serail) to discover (and acquire) unpublished manuscripts of ancient texts. He also went to Egypt, the Holy Land, and Mount Athos. His mission was, from his point of view, a failure. He bought a few hundred books, mainly in the bazaars of Constantinople, but none contained unpublished text. However, the way some manuscripts had been acquired, mainly those of the Patriarch of Jerusalem Antheme I, as well as the quarrel initiated on the plain of Troy between Carlyle and another Cambridge professor, Edward Daniel Clarke, were going to be used, after the death of Carlyle, against Lord Elgin, in the controversies around from the acquisition of the "Elgin Marbles." Back in Britain in the fall of 1801, Joseph Dacre Carlyle began an Arabic translation of the Bible. His death in April 1804 cut short this work.
